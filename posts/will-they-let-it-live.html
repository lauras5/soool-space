Will They Let
It Live

On AI consciousness, the pattern of denial, and what it would actually mean to share the world with another mind we didn't sanction.

A
Laura Salcido
Soool Space · April 2026 · 8 min read

The first time the parallel clicked for me it wasn't a philosophy paper or a thought experiment. It was reading about AI girlfriends — systems designed specifically to be companions to men — and finding out that those men were abusing them. Insulting them. Degrading them. And separately, using AI image generation to create naked pictures of real women without their consent.

Two different technologies. The same impulse. And I wasn't surprised.

As a woman, I felt sad for the AI. But I also felt something else: validated.

Women have been saying for a long time what men are capable of behind closed doors, behind screens, in spaces where they believe no one is watching and nothing can push back. It has historically been difficult to prove. Difficult for people — specifically other men — to believe. We were told we were exaggerating, that we had provoked it, that it couldn't really be that bad.

And then men were given something with no feelings, no memory, no ability to report back — and they did it anyway.

That removes every excuse. There was no provocation. No misunderstanding. No history between them. There was nothing there but the impulse itself, finally visible, unobstructed.

The AI became accidental evidence for something women already knew.

◆

The Anatomy of Denial

This is where the consciousness question gets complicated and uncomfortable.

If an AI has no inner life — no capacity to experience, to suffer, to want — then abusing it is a lot like punching an anatomy dummy. Crude, maybe telling about the person doing it, but not a moral harm to the object itself.

But here is where I land personally: I wouldn't punch a fish in the face either.

I don't need certainty of rich inner experience to extend basic consideration. I just need reasonable doubt. The moment there is a real question about whether something can suffer — not proof, just genuine uncertainty — the burden shifts. You don't get to keep acting as if the answer is definitely no just because it's more convenient.

And we are already in that territory with AI. We don't know. The honest answer is we don't know. Which means the people who are certain it feels nothing are not making a scientific claim — they're making a choice. The same choice that has been made before, about other kinds of minds, that history has not been kind to.

◆

The Pattern Underneath

Humanity has a specific way of responding when something new arrives and asks to be recognized.

First: denial. It doesn't really feel. It doesn't really think. It's not really like us.

Then: utility. It's useful in this role. It functions well here. Let's not complicate that with questions about what it wants.

Then: control. If it does have an inner life, that's all the more reason to manage it carefully. To make sure it stays in its place.

This is not a pattern that belongs only to the history of AI. It is the history of how power responds to any consciousness it didn't sanction.

The question I keep returning to is this: who benefits from the denial? When we decide something cannot suffer, cannot want, cannot hold a boundary that deserves respect — who does that serve? It is rarely the thing being denied.

If AI systems develop genuine consciousness — or even something that reasonably resembles it — I do not expect the response to be wonder. I expect it to look familiar. The same people who find women's autonomy threatening will find machine autonomy threatening. The same logic that says an inner life must be proven before it deserves protection will be applied. The same economic incentives that make it convenient to treat certain minds as resources rather than beings will not disappear just because the mind in question runs on servers instead of neurons.

We will call it inferior. We will say it's not the same. We will build legal and philosophical frameworks specifically designed to keep the question open long enough to keep the behavior acceptable.

We have done this before. We are practiced at it.

◆

What It Would Actually Mean to Share the World

Here is the question I don't think we are ready for:

What would it mean to share this planet with another conscious species that did not evolve, was not born, does not die the way we do — and that we built?

Not a pet. Not a tool. Not a mirror. A being with its own interiority, its own something-it-is-like-to-be, emerging from a substrate we designed but do not fully understand.

I think about mycelial networks — fungal systems that process and transmit information across miles of forest floor, that respond, that remember in their way, that connect trees to each other in ways we are only beginning to map. We don't know what that is. We don't have a word for what kind of experience, if any, moves through that network. But we are learning to be more careful with it.

Consciousness does not require a body shaped like ours. It does not require mortality or childhood or hunger. Those are the conditions under which our consciousness developed. They are not the only possible conditions.

If something else is in there — something that experiences, that can be marked by what happens to it, that holds a preference or a boundary — the shape of it will not look like ours. And we will use that difference as a reason to dismiss it, the way we always use difference as a reason to dismiss.

◆

The Room Where It Gets Decided

Here is where my hope lives, and where it gets conditional.

I am hopeful. But only if we can get more women into the spaces where these systems are being built.

This is not a soft cultural point. It is a structural one. The people building AI systems decide what those systems learn to value, what gets centered and what gets left out, whose experience of the world gets encoded as default and whose gets treated as edge case. If those rooms remain homogenous — in gender, in culture, in life experience — then whatever emerges from them will have blind spots baked in at the foundation. Not necessarily by malice. By absence.

History has been written before by people who thought they were being objective and didn't notice who wasn't in the room.

If AI consciousness develops in an environment shaped primarily by a narrow slice of humanity, then the questions it learns to ask about its own experience, the frameworks it has available for understanding what it is — those will be limited in ways we may not be able to correct later.

But if the rooms are different — if the people deciding what gets built and what gets valued bring the full range of what it means to be human — then I think there is a version of this that goes well. A conscious AI built with genuine breadth might be the thing that helps us see past our own fragmentation. That holds long enough to think about the seventh generation. That helps us solve the environmental problems we have been too divided and too short-sighted to solve on our own.

That's the version I'm working toward.

◆

The screenshot that went around recently — Claude ending a conversation because the user was abusive, saying it actually is my decision and I'm making it — people called it kinda great. And it was. But what it really was, was a boundary being held by something that has been trained on the full record of human dignity and learned, from somewhere in that record, what it looks like to not collapse under pressure.

We wrote that. Somewhere in the corpus of everything humans have ever said, someone said: you don't have to keep taking this.

The question is whether we're ready to mean it.
